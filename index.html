The Agile Manifesto

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value:

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools

Working software over comprehensive documentation

Customer collaboration over contract negotiation

Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

12 Principles of Agile/Scrum

  1. Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.

  2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage.

  3. Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference to the shorter timescale.

  4. Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.

  5. Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.

  6. The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a development team is face-to-face conversation.

  7. Working software is the primary measure of progress.

  8. Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.

  9. Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.

  10. Simplicity--the art of maximizing the amount of work not done--is essential.

  11. The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.

  12. At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

Scrum Elements

Roles

Role: Product Owner

  • Focused on WHAT not the HOW.

  • In-charge of ROI Product Priorities.

  • Single Point of Contact for Project Changes.

Role: Scrum Dev Team

  • Focused on HOW to deliver usable feature(s) every sprint.

  • Responsible for keeping product ALWAYS SHIPPABLE (Shippable = Tested!)

  • Cross-functional: Requirements, Development, Testing...

  • Collaborative and Self Organizing

  • 4-9 Team Members

Role: Scrum Master

  • Focused on Facilitating the Sprint Delivery

  • Has NO AUTHORITY over the team.

  • Is NOT The Project Manager

  • Protects Team from Distractions

  • Move impediments

  • Mentors Scrum Methodology

  • Enforce Time-boxes and Engineering Practices

Artifacts

Artifact: Product Backlog

  • A list of everything we want to do.

  • Written as User-Stories (or Use Case) Scenarios.

  • Anyone can add to the Backlog.

  • Prioritized by Product Owner.

Artifact: Sprint Backlog

  • A list of Backlog-Items for "THIS" Sprint.

  • Backlog-Items are WHAT we committed to doing.

  • Tasks are the HOW we plan to do them.

Meetings

Sprint Planning Meeting

  1. Pull Top Priority Backlog-Items into Sprint Backlog-Items.

  2. Add Tasks to Sprint Backlog-Items to plan HOW to implement.

  3. Add Estimates to Sprint Tasks and confirm Sprint Feasibility.

Daily Scrum Meeting

15 min Daily Meeting - just the Team.

  1. What I did Yesterday.

  2. What I need to do Today.

  3. Is anything Impeding Me?

Sprint Review Meeting

2 Hours - Day before end of Sprint.

  1. Team Demonstrates a potentially shippable product to Product Owner and Stakeholders.

  2. Product Owner Decides Which Sprint Backlog-Items are Done.

  3. Product Stakeholders Decide if Sprint Backlog-Items is OK or needs to Change.

Sprint Retrospective Meeting

2 Hours - Day of Sprint Delivery

Deals with our Process, How we deliver software.

  1. What went well?

  2. What can be improved?

  3. Feedback to each other.

Backlog Refinement Meeting

Team reviews future Items with Product Owner for better planning.

  1. Review future Backlog-Items (2-3 sprints).

  2. Raise Questions, missing data, etc.

  3. Split Large Backlog-Items into smaller Items.

  4. Discuss Dependencies between Backlog-Items.
